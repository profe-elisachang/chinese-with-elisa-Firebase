Chinese Conversation Self-Study Guide

Topic: Daily Life, Family & Future Plans | Level: Intermediate

Objective: Understand a natural Chinese dialogue and the cultural ideas behind it.

The Complete Dialogue

Context: Two friends (A and B) meet and chat about their day and their children.

A: 昨天你去书店了没有？
A: Zuótiān nǐ qù shūdiàn le méiyǒu?
Translation: Did you go to the bookstore yesterday?

Language & Culture Notes:

  • 了没有 (le méiyǒu): A common way to form a yes/no question about a past action. It's like asking "Did you... or not?"
  • Cultural Start: Chinese casual conversations often start with a specific, neutral activity (like going to a bookstore). It's a safe and natural opener.
B: 我没去书店，我去理发了。
B: Wǒ méi qù shūdiàn, wǒ qù lǐfà le.
Translation: I didn't go to the bookstore, I went to get a haircut.

Language & Culture Notes:

  • 没 (méi) + Verb: The standard way to negate a past action. "我去书店了" (I went) -> "我没去书店" (I didn't go).
  • 理发 (lǐfà): Specifically means "to get a haircut" (the service), not just to cut hair in general.
A: 我也去理发了，我在理发店门口怎么没见到你啊！
A: Wǒ yě qù lǐfà le, wǒ zài lǐfàdiàn ménkǒu zěnme méi jiàndào nǐ a!
Translation: I also got a haircut! How come I didn't see you at the door of the barbershop?

Language & Culture Notes:

  • 怎么没...啊 (zěnme méi... a): Expresses surprise or confusion about why something didn't happen. "How come...?"
  • Finding Common Ground: Speaker A immediately latches onto the shared activity ("我也..."). This builds connection in Chinese conversation.
B: 你是上午去的还是下午去的？
B: Nǐ shì shàngwǔ qù de háishì xiàwǔ qù de?
Translation: Did you go in the morning or the afternoon?
A: 我从来不在上午理发，我是下午去的。
A: Wǒ cónglái bù zài shàngwǔ lǐfà, wǒ shì xiàwǔ qù de.
Translation: I never get haircuts in the morning, I went in the afternoon.

Language & Culture Notes:

  • 从来不在 (cónglái bù zài): "Never (do something) at/in..." A strong statement of personal habit.
  • 是...的 (shì... de): This structure is used to emphasize details (time, place, manner) of a past action. Here, it emphasizes "in the afternoon."
B: 听说，你孩子大学要毕业了，是吗？
B: Tīngshuō, nǐ háizi dàxué yào bìyè le, shì ma?
Translation: I heard your child is about to graduate from university, is that right?

Language & Culture Notes:

  • 听说 (tīngshuō): "I heard that..." A crucial phrase for polite conversation. It shows your topic isn't random prying, but comes from general knowledge, making it less intrusive.
  • ..., 是吗? (..., shì ma?): Tag question meaning ", is that right?" Used to confirm information gently.
  • Cultural Shift: This is the natural transition from small talk (haircut) to the core Chinese social topic: children's education and future.
A: 是的，他今年七月份毕业。
A: Shì de, tā jīnnián qīyuè fèn bìyè.
Translation: Yes, he's graduating this July.
B: 你孩子学文科还是理科？
B: Nǐ háizi xué wénkē háishì lǐkē?
Translation: Does your child study liberal arts or sciences?

Language & Culture Notes:

  • 文科 (wénkē) / 理科 (lǐkē): A fundamental division in the Chinese education system. This is often one of the first questions about a student.
  • Cultural Insight: This question reflects the deep-rooted "Arts vs. Sciences" tracking in Chinese society.
A: 他学理科，他数学和化学的成绩不错，考试都在九十分以上，他不喜欢上文化课。
A: Tā xué lǐkē, tā shùxué hé huàxué de chéngjī bùcuò, kǎoshì dōu zài jiǔshí fèn yǐshàng, tā bù xǐhuān shàng wénhuàkè.
Translation: He's in sciences. His math and chemistry grades are pretty good, his exam scores are all above 90. He doesn't like "culture classes" (liberal arts subjects).

Language & Culture Notes:

  • 成绩不错 (chéngjī bùcuò): A common, modest way to say "grades are good."
  • 九十分以上 (jiǔshí fèn yǐshàng): "Above 90 points." In China's百分制 (100-point scale), 90+ is excellent.
  • 文化课 (wénhuàkè): In this context, it specifically refers to liberal arts courses (like Chinese, history) for science students. It highlights the arts/sciences divide.
B: 他怎么不喜欢学文科呢？
B: Tā zěnme bù xǐhuān xué wénkē ne?
Translation: How come he doesn't like studying liberal arts?
A: 他不喜欢作文，外语口试和笔试的成绩不好。
A: Tā bù xǐhuān zuòwén, wàiyǔ kǒushì hé bǐshì de chéngjī bù hǎo.
Translation: He doesn't like writing compositions, and his grades for foreign language oral and written exams aren't good.

Language & Culture Notes:

  • 作文 (zuòwén): Essay/composition writing, a core and often dreaded part of Chinese language classes.
  • 口试 (kǒushì) / 笔试 (bǐshì): Oral exam / Written exam.
  • Pattern: The parent gives very specific, score-based reasons for the child's preference. This is typical in Chinese discussions about academics.
B: 大学本科毕业以后，他想考研究生吗？
B: Dàxué běnkē bìyè yǐhòu, tā xiǎng kǎo yánjiūshēng ma?
Translation: After graduating with his bachelor's degree, does he want to take the exam for graduate school (master's)?

Language & Culture Notes:

  • 考研究生 (kǎo yánjiūshēng): "To take the entrance exams for postgraduate studies." It's a competitive process.
  • Natural Progression: This is a very logical next question in a Chinese context, showing concern for the child's academic progression.
A: 当然想了，大学一毕业就考上研究生，这是最理想的了。现在岁数不大，以后岁数大了，再想考也考不了了。
A: Dāngrán xiǎng le, dàxué yī bìyè jiù kǎoshàng yánjiūshēng, zhè shì zuì lǐxiǎng de le. Xiànzài suìshu bù dà, yǐhòu suìshu dà le, zài xiǎng kǎo yě kǎo bù liǎo le.
Translation: Of course he does. Getting into grad school right after university graduation is the most ideal situation. He's not old now, but when he gets older later, even if he wants to take the exam, he won't be able to.

Language & Culture Notes:

  • 一...就... (yī... jiù...): "As soon as... then..." Emphasizes immediacy and seamless transition.
  • 最理想的了 (zuì lǐxiǎng de le): "The most ideal." The "的了" ending adds strong emphasis and a tone of conclusion.
  • Cultural Pressure: This expresses a common "age clock" anxiety in Chinese education/career planning. There's pressure to achieve milestones (like grad school) at the "right" age.
B: 将来他想干什么工作？
B: Jiānglái tā xiǎng gàn shénme gōngzuò?
Translation: What kind of job does he want to do in the future?
A: 他想先在商业部门工作几年，然后去大学当老师。他常跟我说，当老师好是好，就是钱少。
A: Tā xiǎng xiān zài shāngyè bùmén gōngzuò jǐ nián, ránhòu qù dàxué dāng lǎoshī. Tā cháng gēn wǒ shuō, dāng lǎoshī hǎo shì hǎo, jiùshì qián shǎo.
Translation: He wants to work in the business sector for a few years first, then go to a university to be a teacher. He often tells me, "Being a teacher is good and all, it's just that the money is low."

Language & Culture Notes:

  • 先...然后... (xiān... ránhòu...): "First... then..." A classic structure for describing plans.
  • 好是好，就是... (hǎo shì hǎo, jiùshì...): A key conversational pattern. It means "[Something] is good, that's true, BUT..." It shows a balanced, practical evaluation, acknowledging both pros and cons.
  • Cultural Value: This reflects a common career dilemma: the stability and respect of a teaching job vs. the higher income of a business job.
B: 他想不想出国留学？
B: Tā xiǎng bu xiǎng chūguó liúxué?
Translation: Does he want to go study abroad?
A: 现在哪个男孩儿不想出国留学啊！
A: Xiànzài nǎge nánháir bù xiǎng chūguó liúxué a!
Translation: What boy nowadays doesn't want to study abroad?!

Language & Culture Notes:

  • 哪个...不...啊 (nǎge... bù... a): A rhetorical question meaning "Which [person] doesn't...?!" It's used to strongly affirm that everyone wants/has this thought.
  • 男孩儿 (nánháir): "Boy," used here informally to mean "young men."
  • Cultural Sentiment: This expresses the very common and strong aspiration for overseas education among Chinese families, seen as a path to better opportunities.
B: 他想到国外去学什么专业？
B: Tā xiǎng dào guówài qù xué shénme zhuānyè?
Translation: What major does he want to study overseas?
A: 他开始跟我说，他想去学数学，后来又说，想去国外学习商业，还说，将来想当经理。
A: Tā kāishǐ gēn wǒ shuō, tā xiǎng qù xué shùxué, hòulái yòu shuō, xiǎng qù guówài xuéxí shāngyè, hái shuō, jiānglái xiǎng dāng jīnglǐ.
Translation: At first he told me he wanted to study math, but later he said he wants to study business abroad, and also said he wants to be a manager in the future.

Language & Culture Notes:

  • 开始...后来... (kāishǐ... hòulái...): "At first... later..." Used to describe a change of mind or plan over time.
  • 还说 (hái shuō): "And also said..." Adds another related point.
  • Ending Note: The dialogue ends on the child's evolving, aspirational plans, which is a positive and forward-looking way to conclude such a conversation.

Self-Study Summary & Key Takeaways

Conversation Flow & Culture:

  • Structure: Specific small talk → Finding common ground → Transition to core family topic (child's future).
  • Core Chinese Social Topics Covered: Child's academic performance (scores!), choice of major (Arts/Sciences), postgraduate plans, career goals, and studying abroad.
  • Parental Tone: The parent (Speaker A) speaks with a mix of pride (good grades), practicality (discussing money), and concern for the "ideal path."

Must-Learn Sentence Patterns:

  • Polite Inquiry: 听说...[Topic]..., 是吗？ (I heard...[Topic]..., is that right?)
  • Giving Balanced Opinion: [Thing] 好是好，就是... ([Thing] is good, but...)
  • Strong Affirmation (Rhetorical): 哪个...[Person]...不...[Verb]...啊！ (What [Person] doesn't [Verb]...?!)
  • Emphasizing Past Detail: 是 + [Detail] + 去的/的 (It was [Detail] that I did it.)

Practice on Your Own:

  • Try to write or speak about a friend's or your own educational background and future plans using the vocabulary and patterns from this dialogue.
  • Replace the topics (e.g., change "study abroad" to "find a job," change "business" to "computer science").
